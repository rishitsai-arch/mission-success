                        Beginning of Savitri Bhai Phule

Savitribai’s early career was defined by radical resilience. She treated education not as a gift she was giving to the poor, but as a weapon she was handing to the oppressed to fight their own battles.

Savitribai Phule’s career began not as a traditional profession, but as a calculated act of social defiance. Her journey started with a period of intense, clandestine apprenticeship under her husband, Jyotirao Phule, who recognized her intellectual potential and taught her to read and write at home. To professionalize her mission, she sought formal education at Mrs. Mitchell’s school and Ms. Farar’s Institution, effectively becoming one of India's first trained female teachers. This strategic preparation culminated on January 1, 1848, when she co-founded India's first school for girls at Bhide Wada in Pune. At just 17 years old, she wasn't merely teaching literacy; she was implementing a revolutionary curriculum centered on rationalism and science, aimed at dismantling the deep-seated caste and gender hierarchies of the time.

The depth of her commitment was most visible in her daily commute, which she turned into a masterclass in non-violent resistance. Faced with a local community that pelted her with mud and stones to halt her progress, she famously carried a spare saree in her bag to change into once she reached the school, ensuring her dignity remained as intact as her resolve. Beyond the classroom, Savitribai acted as a social entrepreneur, introducing stipends to prevent girls from dropping out due to poverty and organizing parent-teacher meetings to advocate for the value of female education. By 1851, her "startup" for social justice had expanded to three schools, proving that her career was built on a foundation of radical resilience and the belief that education was a weapon for the oppressed to claim their own human rights.
